Types of Methodologies

Waterfall Model

Pro's Cons
  • Produces large quantities of information for review, which may be useful for modifying or bugfixing down the line
  • Organising tasks and reviewing progress is easy
  • A simple model, with clearly defined stages that are simple to follow and coordinate
  • The fact that such vast quantities of information are documented can slow the process down and impair the reviewing process
  • This method does not easily support drastic re-design of features, as much of the progress achieved will be lost rolling back the various stages of development
  • Any misinterpretations of client needs that are not discovered early on can severely impair the process of development
  • Distinct lack of flexibility in terms of changing requirements, which when concerning modern software development and the rapid advance of technology, is a crippling flaw
  • Client/User testing is only implemented at the testing stage, so any serious issues accrued previously will not be identified until this stage, which can sverely delay projects incurring heavy costs and waste of time
  • It is not until late in development that any working software is released, so getting an accurate idea of how a project is developing previous to this is difficult
  • Often disregards the need for efficiency, which can impair user experience and future modification

Rapid-Application-Development

Pro's Cons
  • Suited to vague and adaptable requirements
  • Results in superior user experience
  • Changing needs and requirements are easily integratable into the process
  • Customer feedback is well accommodated
  • Efficient code is not often regarded as important
  • Heavily reliant on client/user feedback

Agile Method: Extreme Programming (XP)

Pro's Cons
  • Produces very high quality code
  • Consistent testing
  • All programmers follow the same basic concepts and guidelines, allows for better collaboration
  • Programmers and designers work at a steady but not overwhelming pace, reduces fatigue, and therefore reduces risk of mistakes and delays
  • Programmers need to be within a local range of each other to coordinate effectively
  • Customer must provide a representative with which to consistently assess product

RAD - The Spiral Model

Pro's Cons
  • Suited to projects with high risk and complexity
  • Results in superior user experience
  • Changing needs and requirements are easily integratable into the process
  • User's gain access to content early on in the development cycle
  • Efficient code is not often regarded as important
  • Management of this method is much more complex - will incur higher costs
  • It is in general a more complex process
  • Risk analysts are reccommended, but will be costly
